Jan 1, 0001

Open science aims to make science more reachable by a broader community. This can be a goal for practical and altruistic reasons. One argument of open science is that any research that is funded by tax payer dollars should be available to all taxpayers. Open research and data sharing can have many benefits to the research as well. It can signal to the broader science community that their work is credible and reproducible. This can benefit the researcher by broadening the influence of their work by allowing it to be obtained by more people in a more transparent way. While open access journals sometimes have higher processing fees then subscription journals, many have no fees at all. Another benefit of publishing in open access journals is that researcher maintain the rights to their work where it is usually transferred to the journal it is published in.

Not only is open science being adopted by researchers it is often being required by funders. Open science can help researchers limit error, increase reproducibility, find more collaborators, increase their number of citations, as well as lower the personal cost of publishing research.

Currently many universities access the quality of a researchers work by the journal impact factor (IF) of the journal they publish in. This can go against many open science practices. This is because while more open access journals are getting larger IF’s subscription journals such as Nature and Science have higher IF’s and higher prestige. Another barrier to the adoption of open science is the hesitance of the researcher to participate in open science depending on their career stage and income. Young researchers may also be penalized for participating in open science as they will have less publication in journals with high IF’s so their job bargaining can be lowered. Open science also has the potential to increase bias in the review process as minority researcher maybe be discriminated against when double blind standards are removed in the peer review process. Open science has the goal of making science accessible to everyone (producer and consumer) but the current mechanics of open science still pose threats especially to researchers who are early in their careers or who may face minority discrimination.

In my current work I am attempting to make my research more open source by posting all code to GitHub (unless it is data that is sensitive to my client). In my groups current work with soil carbon we are ensuring that all producers and research institutes that we are gleaning data from get final outputs and results especially as they pertain to their production.

The limitations of my current open science practices is ensuring that I am not sharing data that is sensitive to our client. With this said as I move away from UCSB I would like to collaborate within industry and share environmental metric data to improve the industry as a whole

McKiernan, E.C. et al. (2016). How open science helps researchers succeed. eLife. 2016; 5: e16800. doi: 10.7554/eLife.16800.
